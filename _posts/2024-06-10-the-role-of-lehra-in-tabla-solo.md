If you've ever watched a tabla solo performance, you've noticed the melodic accompaniment playing continuously in the background — that's the lehra (also called nagma). Far from being mere background music, the lehra plays a crucial role in shaping the entire performance.

## More Than a Metronome

While the lehra does serve a timekeeping function, reducing it to a metronome misses the point entirely. The lehra is a melodic partner in a rhythmic conversation. It provides:

- **Taal reference** — The lehra melody cycles through the taal, giving both performer and audience a continuous reference for where sam falls
- **Emotional context** — The raag of the lehra sets the emotional tone of the performance
- **Contrast** — The steady melody provides a canvas against which the tabla's rhythmic creativity stands out

## The Art of Playing Against the Lehra

Experienced tabla players develop a sophisticated relationship with the lehra. They might:

- Play phrases that deliberately create rhythmic tension before resolving on sam
- Use the lehra's melodic phrases as cues for their own compositions
- Vary their dynamics in response to the lehra's contour

This interplay is what makes a tabla solo a *musical* experience rather than a purely rhythmic one.

## Choosing the Right Lehra

The choice of raag and taal for the lehra is an artistic decision that shapes the entire performance:

- **Yaman** — Bright, evening raag; works beautifully with Teentaal
- **Bhairavi** — Versatile and emotional; often used for the concluding piece
- **Khamaj** — Sweet and accessible; popular for lighter compositions
- **Des** — Romantic, late-evening mood; pairs well with many taals
