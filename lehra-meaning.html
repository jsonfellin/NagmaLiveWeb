What is Lehra?

Understanding Nagma — the melodic backbone of Indian classical music practice.

Lehra: The Melodic Cycle

Lehra (also spelled Lehera, or called Nagma) is a melodic composition played in a fixed rhythmic cycle (taal) that serves as an accompaniment for tabla solos, Kathak dance performances, and vocal recitals in Indian classical music.

The lehra provides a continuous melodic reference point, helping the performer stay oriented within the rhythmic cycle. Think of it as a melodic compass — while the tabla player explores complex rhythmic patterns and improvisations, the lehra steadily marks the taal, always returning to sam (the first beat).

How is Lehra Used?

Lehra serves several important purposes in Indian classical music:

  • Tabla solo accompaniment — During a tabla solo, the lehra provides the melodic backdrop against which the tabla player improvises, enabling the audience to follow the rhythmic structure.
  • Kathak dance — Kathak dancers rely on the lehra to maintain the taal cycle while performing intricate footwork patterns (tatkar) and compositions (tukra, toda).
  • Practice aid — Students of all instruments use lehra to practice staying in taal, developing their sense of laya (tempo) and sam awareness.
  • Performance — In concerts, a harmonium or sarangi player often performs the lehra live, but increasingly musicians turn to high-quality digital alternatives for practice.

Instruments That Play Lehra

Traditionally, lehra is performed on:

  • Harmonium — The most common lehra instrument, especially in North Indian classical music.
  • Sarangi — A bowed string instrument that provides a rich, expressive lehra.
  • Violin — Sometimes used for lehra, particularly in South Indian contexts.
  • Sitar or Sarod — Occasionally used for lehra in certain styles.

Lehra and Raag

Every lehra is composed in a specific raag (melodic framework). The choice of raag for a lehra depends on the mood and context of the performance. Common raags used for lehra include Yaman, Bhairavi, Khamaj, Bilawal, and Kafi, though lehra compositions exist in dozens of raags.
